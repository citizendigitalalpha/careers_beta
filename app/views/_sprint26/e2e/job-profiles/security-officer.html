How to become

You could develop the skills you need for this role by:

  • studying at university or college
  • doing an apprenticeship
  • getting paid or voluntary work experience

When you're applying for work, you'll need to have evidence of your skills and technical abilities through a portfolio, or online testimonies.

University

You could do a foundation degree, Higher National Diploma (HND) or degree. Relevant subjects include:

  • web design and development
  • computer science
  • digital media development
  • software engineering

Requirements

You'll usually need:

  • 2 to 3 A levels for a degree

More information

  • UCAS has information on degree courses and entry requirements.
  • You can apply for student finance to cover fees and living costs.

College

You could do a level 2, 3 or 4 course in web development. These can last from a few months to a year.

Requirements

You'll usually need:

  • 5 GCSEs at grades 9 to 4 (A* to C) including English and maths

More information

  • You may be able to get financial help to do a course.
  • Find a web development course near you.

Apprenticeship

There are 3 levels of apprenticeship you could do in IT, software, web and telecoms professionals. These are intermediate, advanced and higher apprenticeship. These could take up to 2 and a half years to complete.

Alternatively, if you have A levels, you could go straight into a higher apprenticeship lasting 18 months.

Requirements

You'll usually need:

  • 5 GCSEs at grades 9 to 4 (A* to C) including English and maths
  • 2 to 3 A levels for a higher apprenticeship

More information:

  • You may be able to use equivalent level qualifications to get onto a course.

Work your way up

You could get a job in IT at any level and then work your way up. You could learn on the job, or take part-time qualifications online or at college.

You could also get the skills you need by volunteering to set up a website for a charity or an event.

More information

  • Tech Future Careers has more information on tech roles including web development.
  • Find out more about getting into a digital career from the Bubble Digital Career Portal.

What it takes

Skills and knowledge

You'll need:

  • problem-solving skills
  • concentration skills
  • customer service skills
  • speaking and listening skills
  • the ability to use English - reading, writing, spelling and grammar skills

Restrictions

You'll need

  • to pass identity and enhanced criminal records checks

    An employer will carry out checks on your personal and work history (up to 10 years). You need to let them know about any criminal convictions before you apply for a licence.

  • a Security Industry Authority (SIA) licence for agency and contractor jobs.

    You won't need an SIA licence for in-house security jobs at your employer's premises.

  • If you work as a contractor on a building site, you'll also need a Construction Skills Certification Scheme card (CSCS).

  • To be aged 18 or over

What you'll do

Your role will vary depending on the industry you work in, and could include:

  • patrolling, securing and monitoring premises
  • guarding cash or other valuables in a security vehicle
  • airport security including searching aircraft, passengers and luggage, and guarding the airport boundaries
  • supervising on the door for a large organisation

Career path and progression

With experience, you could progress to senior or chief security officer, then supervisor.

With further qualifications you could go into security management or training, or set up your own security company.
