Veterinary nurse

Veterinary nurses work with vets to provide nursing care for sick and injured animals.

Salary: £18,000 to £26,000 per year
Hours: 35 to 40 per week

1. How to become a veterinary nurse

There are 2 main routes into veterinary nursing:

  • degree, or a foundation degree, in veterinary nursing
  • level 3 diploma in veterinary nursing

University

To get onto an honours degree in veterinary nursing you’ll need at least:

  • 5 GCSEs at grades 9 to 4 (A* to C), including maths, English language and science
  • 2 A levels, including biology or chemistry

Universities may accept equivalent level 3 qualifications.

To get a foundation degree in veterinary nursing you’ll need at least:

  • 5 GCSEs at grades 9 to 4 (A* to C), including maths, English language and science
  • 2 A levels, including biology

You can find out about universities and entry requirements from UCAS.

You may be able to get a student loan to help with the cost of a course.

College

You can do a Level 3 Diploma in Veterinary Nursing at college. The Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons (RCVS) has a list of approved training organisations.

You’ll need to have a least 5 GCSEs at grades 9 to 4 (A* to C), including maths, English language and science.

This can be as a full-time course or studying 1 day a week and working in the surgery for 4 days.

You may be able to get financial help to do these courses.

Apprenticeship

Another way into veterinary nursing is through an apprenticeship in veterinary nursing. You could work while training towards qualifications at level 3.

Voluntary or work experience

Relevant work experience may be useful. You could volunteer with a vet, a local kennel or animal welfare centre, or with animal charities like the PDSA or RSPCA.

2. Skills

You'll need:

  • the ability to handle animals in a calm, confident manner
  • practical skills
  • the ability to be tactful and sympathetic with pet owners
  • administration and IT skills

3. What you'll do

You’ll usually work in a general veterinary practice. You'll need to care about animal welfare without being too sentimental.

Your day-to-day tasks may include:

  • speaking to animal owners to find out the problem
  • taking blood and urine samples from animals
  • taking x-rays
  • preparing animals for treatment and assisting vets during treatment
  • giving injections, medication and removing stitches
  • talking to pet owners about how to care for their animals
  • taking care of in-patient animals
  • supervising and helping to train other assistants
  • updating records

In smaller practices you may also work on reception.

4. Salary

Starter: £18,000 to £20,000

Experienced: £21,000 to £24,000

Highly Experienced: £26,000 (senior nurse)

These figures are a guide.

5. Working hours, patterns and environment

You'll usually work 35 to 40 hours a week, with some evenings and weekends.

You'll usually get a uniform and protective clothing.

6. Career path and progression

With experience, you could take on more responsibility, like practice management, supervising and training new staff, or working in veterinary supplies.

You could also train to specialise in working for a zoological/wildlife park, charity, pharmaceutical company or breeding/boarding kennels.

With further study you could work towards becoming a lecturer or researcher.
