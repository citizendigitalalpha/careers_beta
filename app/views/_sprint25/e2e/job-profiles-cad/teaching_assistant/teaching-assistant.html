1. How to become a {{ profileName }}

Each school sets its own entry requirements. Check job vacancies with your local education authority to find out what each school wants.

You could do:

  • a college course
  • an apprenticeship
  • training on the job

You'll usually need:

  • experience of working with children
  • GCSEs at grades 9 to 4 (A* to C) in English and maths, or equivalent qualifications
  • to pass the enhanced background checks your employer or college arrange for you

There is strong competition for jobs, so a qualification in nursery work, childcare, playwork or youth work can give you an advantage.

College

You could study for qualifications like a level 2 or 3 certificate in support for learning and teaching in schools.

Find a course at a college near you.

You may be able to get financial help to do a course.

If you're under 19, these courses are free.

Apprenticeship

You could also get in through an intermediate or advanced apprenticeship in childcare or supporting teaching and learning in schools.

Work

If you don't have any qualifications or experience, you may get into the job if you show you can work well with children, teachers and parents. Many teaching assistants volunteer before getting paid work.

What it takes

You might need:

Technology Skills

  • spreadsheet software
  • word processing software

Knowledge

  • English language - knowledge of spelling and grammar, including the rules of putting words and sentences together

Skills

  • active Listening - giving full attention to what other people are saying
  • speaking - Talking to others to give information effectively
  • understanding human behaviour
  • understanding learning styles - choosing the most suitable way of training or teaching new things

Abilities

  • written comprehension - The ability to read and understand information and ideas presented in writing.

Find other careers with these skills

What you'll do

Your specific day-to-day duties could include:

  • giving extra support to students with special needs, including students whose first language is not English, or students with physical or mental difficulties
  • supervising students in classrooms, halls, dining rooms, playgrounds and on school trips
  • working with children one-to-one or in small groups to support the learning presented by teachers
  • making sure students are following school policies and procedures
  • working closely with classroom teachers
  • giving advise to students on how to use and take care of equipment and materials to prevent injuries and damage
  • observing students' performance, and keeping records to monitor their progress
  • presenting information to students with the help and support of teachers, using lectures, discussions, or role-playing methods
  • preparing lesson materials, displays, equipment, and demonstrations
  • organising and supervising games and activities to promote physical, mental, and social development

Career path and progression

With experience you could move into design team management or expand your skills to become a web content manager.

You might work towards a move into business management.

You could also work as a freelance web designer, or set up your own web design business.
