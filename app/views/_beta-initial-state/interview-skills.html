Even if you have all the right qualifications and experience to do the job, you need to show an employer that you're a good fit for the organisation. This can decide whether or not you get the job.

Interpersonal or soft skills

Employers look for how you interact with the people around you. You will need to demonstrate your 'interpersonal' or ‘soft skills’ during your interview. These can include:

Attitude to work

You need to show that you are hard-working and that like to complete tasks.

If you have had a job before, give examples of how you have shown positive attitudes to your work.

If you're new to work give examples of things such as punctuality and meeting your deadlines.

Positive attitude

Be positive in your interview, smile and speak clearly. Don’t be critical of anything or anyone. Give examples of how you have approached difficult tasks and how you resolved them.

Give clear examples without using jargon or embellishing what you did. This will show that you can think a problem through and that you don’t give up easily.

Body language

Body language can tell an employer a lot about you, if you slouch, fold your arms or put your hands in your pockets, this gives the impression that you don’t care.

Try to be relaxed but sit upright in the chair, look at your interviewer and smile.

Communication skills

Your written communication skills have got you the interview, it's now your chance to show the interviewer that your spoken communication is as strong.

Make sure you practice answers before the day of the interview so you don't end up tongue tied on the day. Don't use slang or street language, make your answers as easy to follow as possible.

Try to give two or three examples when you answer any questions as this will show off your clear spoken English.

Time management

Try to show in your answers that you're punctual and that you don't need to be supervised to complete tasks. Give real examples that show this, either from a previous job or from your school, college or university.

Confidence

You must show you're confident throughout your interview. Remember the basics:

  • shake hands
  • make eye contact
  • smile

It's important not to be so confident that you come across as rude: don't make jokes, swear and always wait to be asked to sit down.

There may be occasions in your interview when it feels as if you're not being interviewed, for example you may be invited to lunch or an informal coffee break.

It's very likely that you're still being interviewed and that people outside of the interview panel such as a receptionist may be asked about how you came across.
