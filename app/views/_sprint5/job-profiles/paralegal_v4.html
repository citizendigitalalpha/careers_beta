1. How to become a paralegal

There are a number of routes to becoming a paralegal. You can:

  • take a degree or a foundation degree
  • do a college course
  • do an apprenticeship
  • start on-the-job training

University

You could study a degree in law, or a higher national diploma or certificate (HND/C) in law, legal studies or paralegal practice.

To get onto a degree you’ll usually need at least 5 GCSEs at grades 9 to 4 (A* to C), including maths and English, and 2 A levels or equivalent level 3 qualifications.

UCAS has information on degree courses and entry requirements.

There’s a lot of competition for places on law degrees, so you’ll need high grades.

For an HND and HNC you’ll usually need 5 GCSEs at grades 9 to 4 (A* to C), including maths and English, or equivalent qualifications(https://www.gov.uk/what-different-qualification-levels-mean/list-of-qualification-levels ), and 1 A level.

You may be able to get financial help to do a higher education course.

College

There are college courses available, including:

  • paralegal practice award, certificate, diploma or higher diploma (level 7)
  • CILEx Level 3 Certificate in Law and Practice
  • legal secretary certificate or diploma
  • an award in legal studies

Entry requirements for these courses vary.

Search for a course.

Apprenticeship

You could take an apprenticeship in legal services.

The Level 3 Advanced Apprenticeship in Legal Services is suitable for school or college leavers.

For the Level 4 Higher Apprenticeship you’ll usually need A levels or relevant experience.

The Chartered Institute of Legal Executives (CILEx) law school has details of training while you work, and about apprenticeships, which can qualify you as a paralegal, a legal executive or a solicitor.

Work

You don’t have to have formal qualifications to be a paralegal, but GCSEs or A levels may help you get on-the-job legal training.

Employers will expect you to have a good standard of English.

You'll need a good understanding of the legal system, so experience working at a law firm, in an admin role for example, could help you start your training.

The Institute of Paralegals has more details about careers.

2. Skills

You'll need:

  • excellent administration, IT and research skills
  • the ability to read and analyse lots of complex information
  • a high level of accuracy and attention to detail
  • the ability to work under pressure and meet deadlines

3. What you'll do

You could work for many kinds of employer, including law firms, private companies, the not-for-profit-sector, local or central government, the police and the courts. You’ll usually specialise in one area of legal work.

Your day-to-day tasks may include:

  • researching and preparing legal documents
  • handling confidential information
  • interviewing clients and witnesses
  • giving clients legal information
  • going to court
  • handling a caseload of clients
  • following instructions
  • general office tasks

4. Salary

Starter: £14,000 to £25,000

Experienced: £30,000 to £40,000

Highly Experienced: £40,000 or more

These figures are a guide.

5. Working hours, patterns and environment

You'll usually work Monday to Friday, 9am to 5pm, in an office. At busy times you may need to work longer. You may travel to meet clients or go to court.

6. Career path and progression

With further study, you could qualify as a legal executive or solicitor.
